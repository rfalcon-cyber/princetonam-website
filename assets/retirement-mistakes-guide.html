Three Common Mistakes That Derail Retirement Plans

A practical guide to the planning blind spots that cost retirees hundreds of thousands — and how to avoid them

Mistake #1: Withdrawal Rates That Fail in Real Markets

The Problem

Most retirees know the "4% rule" — the idea that you can safely withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one, then adjust for inflation each year. It sounds simple. It's dangerously incomplete.

The 4% rule was backtested using historical market data, but it assumes three things that rarely hold in practice:

  • Smooth returns: Real markets don't cooperate. A 40% crash in year one of retirement destroys the math.
  • Predictable inflation: A spike to 8-10% (as we saw in 2022) and your "adjusted" withdrawals become unsustainable.
  • Stable life expectancy: Living to 95 instead of 85 means your portfolio has 10 more years to sustain — and that changes everything.
Example: You retire with $1M, planning 4% withdrawals ($40K/year). Year one brings a 35% market crash. Now you're withdrawing 5.4% of a $650K portfolio instead of 4% of a $1M portfolio. Over time, this compounds into portfolio failure — usually around year 20 of a 30-year retirement.

The Fix

Use dynamic withdrawal rules, not static ones. Model your specific scenario: your actual life expectancy, your actual sequence of returns (including downturns), your actual tax situation, and your actual spending (which often changes in retirement). A 3.5% withdrawal rate with flexibility beats a rigid 4% rule every time.

Mistake #2: Ignoring the Tax Bill That Follows You

The Problem

Retirees accumulate wealth in accounts that were optimized for earning, not for spending. The result: a retirement that looks wealthy on paper but gets hollowed out by taxes.

Three tax disasters converge in retirement:

  • Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs): At 73, the IRS forces you to withdraw from IRAs and 401(k)s, whether you need the money or not. Those withdrawals are taxable income, which can push you into higher brackets and trigger taxes on Social Security benefits.
  • Capital gains in taxable accounts: If your portfolio is heavy in appreciated stocks or mutual funds, each sale triggers capital gains tax — even if you're barely ahead after inflation.
  • Concentrated positions: Many retirees have 40–60% of their wealth in a single stock (inherited, or from a business). Selling it all triggers a massive tax bill. Holding it forever means zero diversification and sleep-loss risk.
Example: A couple retires at 62 with $2M: $1M in retirement accounts, $500K in taxable accounts (mostly appreciated stocks), $500K in tax-free accounts. Without a plan, they withdraw $80K/year. By age 73, RMDs force $120K/year in withdrawals, triggering a tax bill of $30K+. A coordinated strategy—Roth conversions before RMDs kick in, tax-loss harvesting in taxable accounts, strategic charitable giving—cuts that bill in half.

The Fix

Build a tax-coordinated withdrawal strategy before you retire. Map out your account types (pre-tax, after-tax, Roth), your life expectancy, your Social Security timing, and your charitable intentions. Sequence your withdrawals in the order that minimizes lifetime taxes. This alone can save $150K–$400K over a 30-year retirement.

Mistake #3: Social Security Timing That Can't Be Undone

The Problem

Social Security is the foundation of most retirements — predictable, inflation-adjusted income you can't outlive. Yet the timing decision is usually made in a vacuum, without considering the rest of the financial picture.

The stakes are enormous:

  • Claiming at 62 vs. 70: You might collect $1.2M starting at 62, or $1.5M starting at 70. That's not just different timing — it's a 25% higher lifetime benefit if you live past 82.
  • Married couples make it worse: Many couples claim simultaneously, missing the spousal claiming strategy entirely. A couple can often increase lifetime benefits by $200K+ by coordinating their claims.
  • Forgetting about taxes: Up to 85% of Social Security benefits are taxable if you have other income. Combining early Social Security with portfolio withdrawals and RMDs can trigger an effective tax rate above 50%.
Example: A high-earning couple (both age 62) are tempted to claim Social Security immediately to "get theirs" before the system changes. But if one spouse delays to 70 while the other claims at 62, they gain roughly $100K in extra lifetime benefits. If they also coordinate with portfolio withdrawals to minimize tax, they gain another $50K. Total: $150K from a 3-minute decision — yet most couples never have that conversation.

The Fix

Model your Social Security decision as part of an integrated financial plan. Run scenarios: claim at 62, wait to 67, wait to 70. Model each one against your other income, your taxes, your life expectancy, and your spouse's situation. The optimal claim date usually isn't the earliest one — but you won't know unless you look.
