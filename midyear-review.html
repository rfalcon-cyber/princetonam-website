Midyear Market Review & H2 2026 Outlook

Princeton Asset Management — July 2026

A Year in Two Halves

The first half of 2026 brought two surprises that are reshaping how we think about the rest of the year. The market consensus at the start of the year expected the Federal Reserve to begin cutting interest rates by midyear. Instead, the Fed has held rates steady and signaled it may raise them instead. At the same time, a regional conflict that began in late February has pushed oil prices up 60%, driving inflation back toward levels the Fed has been fighting for the past five years.

Below is a summary of what happened, what it means for your portfolio, and what we are watching in the second half.

What Happened in the First Half

The Earnings Story Narrowed, Not Broadened

More than half of all earnings growth this year has come from just five companies investing heavily in AI infrastructure. History suggests this concentration may not persist: when electricity was transformative, the money was made by businesses that used electricity to become more efficient, not by power utilities. Traditional companies—healthcare, financial services, manufacturing—that use AI to cut costs have more room to surprise, especially since they trade at lower valuations.

The Fed Shifted Stance

Markets began 2026 expecting rate cuts. By midyear, the Fed had moved in the opposite direction: nine of eighteen officials now expect at least one rate increase by the end of 2026, with the rest expecting rates to hold steady. Persistent services inflation and the oil shock are the drivers.

What This Means for Your Investments

For the balance of 2026, Princeton expects the Federal Reserve to be on hold. The Federal Reserve cannot credibly lower the interest rate while there are significant hostilities in the Strait of Hormuz and while energy prices are elevated. Core Fixed Income and Credit portfolios will be rewarded for taking on longer Effective Duration. There will come a point in time when the Federal Funds Rate is reduced and duration sensitive bonds will benefit from this.

On the Equities side, Princeton does foresee a broadening of market returns beyond the handful of companies which are driving the S&P 500 Index. A more broad-based participation in the market will likely come from sectors whose past return profiles have been somewhat subdued. These would include Real Estate, Consumer Staples, Consumer Discretionary, Healthcare and, to some extent, the Financials sector.

Market Highs, Sentiment Lows

The University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment Index fell to a record low in the first half of 2026—below the trough of every recession in the survey's 75-year history—yet the S&P 500 has continued to grind higher. Sentiment measures how people feel; markets reflect what investors expect. The index's gains are being driven by an extremely narrow set of companies: only about 17% of S&P 500 stocks outperformed the index over the past month. This is a concentrated bet, not a broad-based growth story.

Our Positioning for the Remainder of 2026

  1. Accepting higher rates. We are comfortable holding short-term Treasury bills and money market funds at 4.5%+ yields, alongside high Effective Duration corporate bonds.
  2. Rotating toward AI adoption, not AI infrastructure. We are gradually increasing exposure to value-oriented U.S. companies and developed international equities that will benefit as AI productivity gains spread beyond the technology sector. Roughly 50% of S&P 500 companies are now actively using AI in day-to-day operations.
  3. Emphasizing quality. With credit spreads at historically tight levels, we are emphasizing investment-grade companies with strong balance sheets, underweighting speculative growth, and avoiding high-yield bonds.

What We Are Watching

The oil price and inflation trajectory, employment trends, the Fed's flexibility if data weakens, and whether AI infrastructure spending is successfully monetized. Any of these could change the outlook, and we are ready to adjust.
