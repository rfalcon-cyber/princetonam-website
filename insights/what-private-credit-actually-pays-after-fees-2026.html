What Private Credit Actually Pays After Fees (2026 Numbers)

Investing · Princeton Asset Management

You've seen the pitch decks: double-digit yields, floating rates, "equity-like returns with debt-like risk." You have real money to allocate, and you suspect the number you'll actually keep is smaller than the number on the cover slide. You're right to suspect it.

This post walks through the arithmetic from gross yield to net-in-your-pocket, using clearly labeled illustrative figures drawn from broad market ranges in early 2026. These are not the returns of any Princeton Asset Management strategy or any specific fund, and they are not predictions.

Start with the gross number

Most direct lending — the core of private credit — is floating rate: a base rate plus a spread. In early 2026, with short-term base rates in the neighborhood of 3.5% to 4%, market commentary has generally put spreads on senior middle-market loans around 4.5 to 6 percentage points, with upfront fees (original issue discount) adding roughly 0.3% to 0.6% per year when amortized.

So a representative gross asset yield might be:

ComponentIllustrative range
Base rate (short-term)3.5% – 4.0%
Credit spread4.5% – 6.0%
Amortized upfront fees0.3% – 0.6%
Gross asset yield≈ 8.5% – 10.5%

That gross figure is what the loans pay. It is not what you get paid. Three things stand between the two: fees, defaults, and cash drag.

Fee drag: management plus incentive

Fund structures vary widely, but market surveys of private credit vehicles commonly show management fees in the range of roughly 1% to 1.5% per year (on invested or net assets), plus an incentive fee — often in the neighborhood of 10% to 15% of income or total return, frequently above a hurdle. Non-traded and feeder structures can add servicing or distribution costs on top. These are illustrative market ranges, not the terms of any particular fund.

Consider a hypothetical fund earning a 9.5% gross yield:

  • Management fee: 1.25% of net assets
  • Incentive fee: 12.5% of income above a 5% hurdle

Incentive math: income above the hurdle is 9.5% − 1.25% − 5.0% = 3.25%; the incentive fee takes 12.5% of the income subject to it. Depending on the exact waterfall, total fee drag in this scenario lands around 2.0% to 2.3% per year. Call it 2.1%.

Running yield after fees: roughly 7.4%. This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome.

Note what happened: fees consumed about a fifth of the gross yield. In a leveraged vehicle, fees charged on gross (levered) assets can consume more of the equity return than the headline percentages suggest — always ask what the fee base is.

Default and loss assumptions: the number everyone skips

Loans default. Over full cycles, middle-market default rates have commonly been discussed in the 1.5% to 3% annual range, with recoveries on senior secured loans historically often near 60–70 cents on the dollar. That implies an annual credit loss of roughly:

2% default rate × 35% loss severity ≈ 0.7% per year, ranging from ~0.5% in benign years to 1.5%+ in stressed ones.

Any projection that assumes zero losses is a marketing document, not an analysis.

Cash drag and the liquidity premium

Two smaller deductions. Committed capital waiting to be called, and cash held for interval-fund repurchases, earns money-market rates rather than loan yields — a drag often worth a few tenths of a percent on the overall allocation.

Against all this, you are being paid a liquidity premium: the extra yield over comparable public credit precisely because you cannot exit freely. Compare the net figure below to broadly syndicated loan or high-yield index yields, which in early 2026 have generally sat in the 7%–8% area with daily liquidity. The gap between that and your private credit net is the price the market is paying you for lockup. If the gap is under a percentage point, ask whether the illiquidity is worth it.

The full walk, in one table

LineIllustrative figure
Gross asset yield9.5%
Less: management + incentive fees−2.1%
Less: expected credit losses−0.7%
Less: cash drag / expenses−0.3%
Illustrative net to investor≈ 6.4%

In a stressed year — defaults at 3.5%, wider losses — that same structure could net 4.5% or less. In a benign year, it could run above 7%. The point is not to predict your outcome. The point is that a 9.5% headline and a mid-6s realistic net are both describing the same investment, and only one of them appears in the brochure.

All figures above are illustrative market-range estimates as of early 2026, subject to change, and do not represent the performance of Princeton Asset Management or any fund.

Remember the tax character

Private credit income is generally taxed as ordinary income. For a high earner at a 37% federal marginal rate plus the 3.8% net investment income tax, an illustrative 6.4% net yield could come to roughly 3.8% after federal tax in a taxable account. Held in an IRA or other qualified account, the picture differs materially. Asset location often matters as much as fund selection here. This is general education, not tax or legal advice. Consult your CPA/attorney.

Questions worth asking before you sign

  1. 1. What is the fee base — committed capital, invested capital, or gross levered assets?
  2. 2. What default and recovery assumptions are behind the projected yield? What was the fund's non-accrual rate last year?
  3. 3. How much leverage does the vehicle use, and what does that do to the downside?
  4. 4. What are the actual repurchase terms, and have repurchases ever been prorated?
  5. 5. What is the net yield spread over a public loan index today — and is that spread paying me enough for the lockup?

The bottom line

Private credit can be a sensible allocation for households that can genuinely leave the capital alone — but the number that matters is net of fees, net of realistic losses, net of cash drag, and, in a taxable account, net of ordinary-income tax. Do the walk from gross to net before you commit, insist on seeing the fund's own version of that walk, and judge the result against what liquid credit pays you for free.
