The Tax Window Between 62 and 65 Worth Six Figures

Retirees · Princeton Asset Management

You retired, or you are about to, somewhere in your early sixties. Your paycheck is gone, Social Security has not started, and required minimum distributions are years away. It feels like a financial no-man's-land — and your quiet worry is that you are missing something expensive while you wait. You are right to wonder. For many retirees, these are the lowest-tax years of their adult lives, and the window closes on a schedule that does not care whether you used it.

Why these years are different

Think about your taxable income at 63. No salary. No bonus. Social Security not yet claimed. RMDs from your IRA not required until 73 under current law. If your spending comes largely from a taxable brokerage account — much of it untaxed return of principal — your reported income can be startlingly low.

A couple who earned $500,000 a year at the peak might show $40,000 of taxable income at 63. That puts them at the bottom of the tax brackets for the first time in decades.

Left alone, that low-income period simply expires. Then, at 73, RMDs begin on an IRA that has kept compounding, Social Security is stacked on top, and the same couple can find themselves pushed back into high brackets permanently — plus Medicare premium surcharges (IRMAA) as a recurring insult.

The gap years are not dead time. They are the one stretch where you get to choose your own tax rate.

The Roth conversion window

A Roth conversion moves money from a traditional IRA to a Roth IRA. You pay ordinary income tax on the converted amount now; in exchange, qualified growth and withdrawals are tax-free later, and Roth IRAs carry no lifetime RMDs for the owner.

The arithmetic case is simple: convert in years when your rate is low, instead of withdrawing later in years when your rate is high.

Using illustrative 2026 figures for a married couple filing jointly (brackets change; confirm current numbers):

  • The 12% bracket runs to roughly $100,000 of taxable income.
  • The 22% and 24% brackets extend to roughly $400,000.
  • The standard deduction shelters about $32,000 before any bracket applies.

A couple with $40,000 of other taxable income could convert roughly $60,000 per year and pay no more than 12% federal on the converted dollars — or convert considerably more and cap out the 22%/24% brackets, if their future rate is likely to be higher than that.

Done annually from 62 to 72, even modest conversions can move $600,000 or more out of the future-RMD pipeline. In this scenario, the difference between converting at 12%–22% now versus withdrawing at 32%-plus later — compounded across a couple's joint life expectancy and their heirs' ten-year inherited-IRA clock — could reach six figures in lifetime tax. It depends on your balances, your rates, and what Congress does. It is not guaranteed. But the order of magnitude is why this window deserves a plan.

The complication: ACA premium subsidies

Here is where retirees who leave work before 65 face a genuine tension. Until Medicare begins at 65, most early retirees buy health insurance on the ACA marketplace, and premium tax credits are based on your modified adjusted gross income — which includes Roth conversions.

Illustrative 2026 figures: unsubsidized marketplace coverage for a 63-year-old couple can run $25,000–$35,000 per year. With income managed low, subsidies can offset a large share of that. And depending on the rules in force for the year (the enhanced-subsidy provisions have changed several times), there can be a hard cliff near 400% of the federal poverty level — roughly $84,000 of MAGI for a couple — where one extra dollar of income could eliminate the entire credit.

So a large Roth conversion at 63 might trigger not just 22% tax but also the loss of $15,000–$20,000 in subsidies. That is an effective marginal rate no one should pay by accident.

The practical resolution is sequencing: in many cases it makes sense to keep income low and harvest subsidies from 62 to 64, then convert aggressively from 65 (Medicare eligibility) until Social Security and RMDs begin. The right split is case-specific and worth modeling rather than guessing.

A worked illustration

Consider a hypothetical couple, both 62, retired, in Florida:

ItemAmount
Traditional IRAs$2,400,000
Taxable brokerage$900,000
Annual spending$130,000
Social Security (deferred to 70)$75,000/yr combined

Ages 62–64: They live on the brokerage account. Reported MAGI is held near $80,000 — under the illustrative subsidy threshold — using dividends, interest, and a small IRA draw. Estimated marketplace subsidy captured: roughly $15,000–$18,000 per year, or $45,000+ over three years.

Ages 65–69: Medicare replaces marketplace coverage; the subsidy constraint disappears. They convert about $180,000 per year to Roth, filling brackets through 24% while watching IRMAA thresholds two years ahead. Over five years, roughly $900,000 moves to Roth at a blended federal cost in the low-20s percent.

Age 70 onward: Social Security starts at its maximized level. At 73, RMDs apply to a much smaller traditional balance. In this scenario, projected RMDs fall from roughly $110,000 to under $60,000 in the first RMD year, keeping the couple in lower brackets, reducing the taxable share of Social Security exposure to IRMAA surcharges, and leaving a tax-free Roth for the surviving spouse and heirs.

Comparing this sequence against doing nothing, the modeled lifetime tax difference in scenarios like this is commonly in the low-to-mid six figures. Your result could be smaller or larger depending on your situation.

This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome.

What to watch

  • IRMAA look-back. Medicare premiums at 65 are based on your tax return from two years prior. Conversions at 63 can raise premiums at 65.
  • Widow's penalty. After a first death, the survivor files single, with compressed brackets. Conversions while both spouses are alive hedge this directly.
  • Paying the conversion tax. Paying it from taxable funds, not from the converted amount, preserves the full benefit.
  • Legislative drift. Brackets, subsidy rules, and RMD ages have all changed in the past decade. Plans should be revisited annually, not set once.

The bottom line

The years between retirement and 65 — and again from 65 until Social Security and RMDs — are a deliberate choice about when you pay tax on decades of deferred savings. Managed passively, the window expires and the IRS sets your rate later. Managed actively, with conversions and subsidy thresholds sequenced correctly, the lifetime difference could reach six figures. The window is open now; it will not reopen.
