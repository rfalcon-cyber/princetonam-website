Case Study: A Physician Couple With $4M — and $600K Less Lifetime Tax

Professionals · Princeton Asset Management

You earn well into the top brackets, save diligently, and still have the sense that your tax bill is quietly larger than it needs to be — not because of one big mistake, but because no one has ever coordinated the pieces. For high-income physicians, that suspicion is usually correct. The cost hides in small, repeated inefficiencies compounded over decades.

What follows is a composite case study. This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome. The couple described is a composite drawn from common situations, and every figure is illustrative, based on 2026 tax parameters that are subject to change. Your numbers would differ.

The couple

Dr. A (52, surgeon) and Dr. B (50, hospitalist) earn a combined $900,000. Their balance sheet:

AssetAmount
Traditional 401(k)s / IRAs$2,400,000
Taxable brokerage$1,300,000
Cash$300,000
Total$4,000,000

They plan to retire at 62 and 60, have two children heading to college, and expect Social Security plus modest part-time income afterward. Their default path: keep maxing pre-tax 401(k)s, hold a tax-inefficient balanced fund in the brokerage account, and take required distributions when forced to.

The problem with the default path is arithmetic. That $2.4 million pre-tax balance, growing at an assumed 6%, could approach $5.5 million by their mid-70s. Required minimum distributions on that balance could exceed $220,000 per year — stacked on Social Security, taxed at 32–35%, and triggering top-tier Medicare surcharges. And if one spouse survives the other, the survivor could pay those rates on nearly the same income with single-filer brackets.

Move 1: A multi-year Roth conversion ladder in the gap years

Between retirement and RMD age, the couple has roughly a decade of low-income "gap years." Suppose they convert $250,000 per year for ten years, deliberately filling the 22% and 24% brackets and managing IRMAA tiers.

Illustrative arithmetic:

  • Converting ~$2.5 million at a blended ~23% effective rate could cost roughly $575,000 in tax, paid from taxable assets.
  • The alternative — leaving those dollars to come out as RMDs and survivor-taxed distributions at a blended 32%+ — could cost roughly $800,000 or more on the same dollars.
  • Illustrative difference: roughly $225,000, before counting decades of tax-free Roth growth thereafter and reduced IRMAA surcharges (plausibly another $40,000–$60,000 over a retirement; call it $50,000).

None of this is guaranteed; it depends on future rates, returns, and law. But the direction of the arithmetic is the point.

Move 2: Backdoor and mega-backdoor Roth during peak earning years

Their income rules out direct Roth IRA contributions. It does not rule out:

  • Backdoor Roth IRAs: $7,000 each per year (illustrative 2026 limit) via nondeductible contribution and conversion — $14,000 per year of new Roth space, provided pre-tax IRA balances are first rolled into their 401(k)s to avoid pro-rata tax.
  • Mega-backdoor Roth: Dr. A's hospital 401(k) allows after-tax contributions with in-plan conversion. Roughly $30,000 per year of additional Roth space.

Call it $44,000 per year for ten working years: about $440,000 contributed that grows tax-free instead of in a taxable account. If those dollars would otherwise have generated taxable dividends and eventual capital gains over 30 years, the avoided drag and back-end tax could plausibly total $130,000–$150,000; use $140,000 as the illustrative midpoint.

Move 3: Asset location — same portfolio, different wrappers

The couple's $1.3 million brokerage account held a balanced fund throwing off ordinary income taxed at 35%–40.8% including NIIT. Reorganizing — municipal bonds and tax-managed equity index exposure in the taxable account, taxable bonds and high-turnover assets inside the retirement accounts — could cut the annual tax drag by an illustrative 0.5% of the taxable balance.

Half a percent on ~$1.3 million, compounded over 25 years of accumulation and drawdown, could amount to roughly $120,000 of avoided tax. Asset location changes what the IRS takes, not what the market gives; it is one of the few adjustments with no required market view.

Move 4: 529 superfunding and early estate positioning

  • 529 plans: Each parent superfunds five years of gifts per child — an illustrative $190,000 per child, $380,000 total. Growth used for education escapes tax entirely; over 8–15 years that could be $40,000–$50,000 of avoided tax on the growth. Call it $45,000.
  • Estate positioning: With $4 million growing toward a potentially taxable estate, annual exclusion gifts and the 529s begin moving appreciation out of the estate early. The Roth conversions also help here: paying income tax now shrinks the taxable estate while leaving heirs an income-tax-free asset — a quiet double benefit under the 10-year inherited-account rule, worth an illustrative $20,000+ to heirs even under conservative assumptions.

Adding it up

MoveIllustrative lifetime tax difference
Roth conversion ladder (rate arbitrage)~$225,000
Reduced IRMAA surcharges~$50,000
Backdoor + mega-backdoor Roth~$140,000
Asset location~$120,000
529 superfunding~$45,000
Estate/inheritance positioning~$20,000
Illustrative total~$600,000

Roughly $600,000 of hypothetical lifetime tax difference — not from any single clever move, but from six ordinary ones, sequenced and coordinated. Each estimate could prove higher or lower depending on returns, tax law, health, and timing. Some moves interact; a careful plan models them together rather than adding them naively, and the true figure in any real situation could be meaningfully different.

Why this couple is typical

Nothing above required exotic products, private deals, or market predictions. It required knowing that gap years exist, that a 401(k) plan document allows after-tax contributions, that pro-rata rules can poison a backdoor Roth, and that the survivor's tax brackets — not the couple's — often govern the endgame. Physicians are busy; these details are precisely what busy people miss.

The bottom line

A hypothetical physician couple with $4 million could see on the order of $600,000 of lifetime tax difference from a coordinated plan: a decade-long Roth conversion ladder in the gap years, backdoor and mega-backdoor contributions while earning, disciplined asset location, and early 529 and estate moves. No single step is dramatic. The compounding of all of them is. Again: this is a hypothetical composite for illustration, not a depiction of any client or a promise of results.
