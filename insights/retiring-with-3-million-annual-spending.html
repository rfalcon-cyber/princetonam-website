Retiring With $3 Million: Exactly How Much You Can Spend Per Year

Retirees · Princeton Asset Management

You have $3 million, or you are close. On paper, you are wealthy. And yet the question that keeps you up at night is oddly simple: if I stop working, how much can I actually spend each year without running out? The fear is not poverty. The fear is being 82, in a down market, and realizing you set the number wrong twenty years earlier.

The honest answer is a range, not a single figure. But it is a narrower range than most people expect, and you can put real numbers on it today.

Start with the withdrawal-rate frameworks

The best-known starting point is the so-called 4% rule. In its original form, it says a retiree who withdrew 4% of the portfolio in year one, then adjusted that dollar amount for inflation annually, historically survived 30-year retirements across most U.S. market periods.

On $3 million, 4% is $120,000 in year one, indexed to inflation thereafter.

Two refinements matter:

  • Conservative planners often use 3.3%–3.5% for longer retirements (35+ years), earlier retirement ages, or lower expected bond and stock returns. On $3 million, that is roughly $99,000–$105,000 per year.
  • Flexible or "guardrails" approaches allow 4.5%–5.5% — but only if you agree, in advance, to cut spending 10%–15% after bad market years. On $3 million, that is $135,000–$165,000, with the understanding that some years you spend less.

So the honest range on $3 million is roughly $100,000 to $165,000 per year, depending on your age, your flexibility, and how much of that spending is truly discretionary.

Why the first decade decides everything

Averages hide the real risk. Two retirees can earn the identical average return over 30 years and end up in completely different places, depending on the order of those returns. This is sequence-of-returns risk.

If a deep bear market hits in years one through five — while you are withdrawing $120,000 a year — you are selling depressed assets to fund groceries. The portfolio may never recover, even if markets do.

Three defenses are worth building before you retire:

  1. 1. A spending reserve. Two to three years of planned withdrawals in short-term, high-quality fixed income, so you are never forced to sell equities into a decline.
  2. 2. Guardrails. A written rule — for example, "if the portfolio falls below $2.5 million, we trim discretionary spending 10%" — decided calmly in advance, not in a panic.
  3. 3. A realistic equity allocation. Enough growth to outpace inflation for 30 years, but not so much that the first bad decade forces the cuts you feared.

This is where portfolio construction and the withdrawal plan have to be designed together, not separately.

The number that matters is after-tax

A $120,000 withdrawal is not $120,000 of spending. What you keep depends heavily on which accounts the money comes from.

  • Traditional IRA / 401(k): Every dollar withdrawn is ordinary income. At a blended federal rate, a married couple pulling $120,000 (with the 2026 standard deduction, illustrative and subject to change) could owe roughly $9,000–$12,000 in federal tax, depending on other income.
  • Roth IRA: Qualified withdrawals are tax-free. $120,000 out is $120,000 to spend.
  • Taxable brokerage: You are taxed only on the gains portion, at long-term capital gains rates — and a married couple with modest other income can realize a meaningful amount of long-term gains at the 0% federal rate. Illustrative figures based on 2026 brackets; these change.

The composition of your $3 million matters as much as the total. A $3 million portfolio that is entirely pre-tax IRA supports noticeably less spending than $3 million split across taxable, Roth, and pre-tax accounts — and the pre-tax version faces required minimum distributions later, whether you need the money or not.

A worked illustration: $3 million at 65

Consider a hypothetical married couple, both 65, retiring in Florida (no state income tax) with:

AccountBalance
Traditional IRA$1,600,000
Taxable brokerage (50% cost basis)$1,000,000
Roth IRA$400,000
Total$3,000,000

They also expect combined Social Security of $60,000 per year starting at 67.

Target spending: $150,000 per year, pre-tax equivalent.

Ages 65–66, before Social Security, they draw about $150,000: roughly $90,000 from the IRA (partly to fill lower tax brackets deliberately) and $60,000 from the brokerage account, where perhaps half is untaxed return of basis. Estimated federal tax in this scenario: roughly $10,000–$13,000.

From 67 on, Social Security covers $60,000, so the portfolio needs to supply only about $90,000 — a 3.0% withdrawal rate on the original balance. That is comfortably inside even the conservative frameworks.

In this scenario, the couple spends close to $150,000 gross per year for life, keeps their portfolio withdrawal rate near or below 4% in every year, and retains the Roth as a late-life and legacy reserve. The sequencing of which account funds which year could reduce their lifetime tax bill materially — depending on their situation.

This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome.

What could change the answer

  • Retiring at 55 instead of 65 stretches the horizon toward 40 years and argues for a rate closer to 3%–3.25% — roughly $90,000–$97,500 on $3 million.
  • Large fixed obligations (a mortgage, tuition support, a dependent adult child) reduce your flexibility, which is what makes higher withdrawal rates survivable.
  • Health care before Medicare can add $15,000–$30,000 per year for a couple, and how you draw income affects premium subsidies.
  • Long-term care. A multi-year care event is the single largest unfunded risk in most plans and deserves its own line in the projection.

The bottom line

On $3 million, sustainable spending sits roughly between $100,000 and $165,000 per year, with $120,000 a defensible center point for a 65-year-old couple. The exact number depends less on the market's average return than on three things you control: how flexible your spending can be, how you defend the first decade against sequence risk, and how intelligently you sequence withdrawals across taxable, pre-tax, and Roth accounts. Set those three deliberately, and the number stops being a guess.
