How Much Do You Really Need for a $200K/Year Retirement?

Retirees · Princeton Asset Management

You have done the mental math in the shower: "We spend about $200,000 a year. So how big does the portfolio have to be before I can stop?" You have probably heard $5 million. You may have heard $7 million. The spread between those answers is a decade of your working life, and nobody seems willing to commit to a number. The fear underneath is specific: retiring two years too early and finding out at 80 — or working five years longer than you ever needed to.

The number is knowable. You just have to build it in the right order: spending, then taxes, then Social Security, then the portfolio.

Step one: define what $200K actually means

First, be precise. Is $200,000 what you want to spend, or what you want the portfolio to pay out? Those differ by the tax bill.

Second, audit the figure. Most pre-retirees quoting a spending number are including costs that end — mortgage principal, 401(k) contributions, payroll taxes, kids' expenses — and excluding ones that begin, like health insurance before Medicare and more travel. Rebuild the number line by line for the retirement years, not the working years. For this article, assume the answer comes back: $200,000 of annual after-tax spending, in today's dollars.

Step two: gross it up for taxes

The portfolio has to produce $200,000 plus whatever the IRS takes, and that depends on which accounts the money comes from.

Illustrative 2026 figures, married filing jointly, Florida residence (no state tax); brackets change annually:

  • All from a traditional IRA: roughly $237,000 of withdrawals to net $200,000 — an effective federal rate near 16% after the standard deduction.
  • All from a taxable brokerage with 50% cost basis: perhaps $212,000–$218,000, since only the gain portion is taxed, at long-term capital gains rates.
  • A blend including Roth: somewhere in between, and often meaningfully better.

A reasonable planning assumption for a typical mixed portfolio is a gross requirement of about $225,000 to spend $200,000. Retirees in high-tax states should add several points more.

Step three: subtract Social Security

The portfolio does not carry the whole load. A high-earning couple both claiming at 67 might receive a combined $70,000–$85,000 per year; delaying to 70 pushes that higher. Use your actual statements from ssa.gov, not a guess.

Assume this couple's combined benefit at 67 is $75,000. Social Security is partially taxable, but conservatively treat it as covering $70,000 of the gross need.

Portfolio's job: $225,000 − $70,000 = roughly $155,000 per year.

That subtraction is the step most people skip, and it is worth well over $1.5 million of required portfolio all by itself.

Step four: apply a withdrawal rate

Divide the portfolio's annual job by a sustainable withdrawal rate:

Withdrawal ratePortfolio needed for $155K/yr
3.25% (early retirement, ~40 yrs)~$4.8 million
4.0% (age 65–67, ~30 yrs)~$3.9 million
4.75% (flexible spending, guardrails)~$3.3 million

So a couple retiring at 67 with normal flexibility needs roughly $3.5–$4 million — not the $5–$7 million a naive "$200K ÷ 4% = $5 million" calculation suggests, because that shortcut ignores Social Security and gets the tax treatment wrong in both directions.

One caveat runs the other way: if you retire at 60, there is a gap before Social Security begins. Those bridge years come entirely out of the portfolio and raise the requirement — often by $500,000 to $1 million.

A worked illustration: the full picture at 66

Consider a hypothetical couple, both 66, planning to retire next year:

InputAmount
Desired after-tax spending$200,000
Estimated blended tax gross-up$25,000 → $225,000 gross
Combined Social Security at 67$75,000 (≈$70,000 net contribution)
Portfolio must supply$155,000/yr
Withdrawal rate assumption4.0%
Indicated nest egg≈$3.9 million

Their actual portfolio: $4.1 million — $2.2 million traditional 401(k)/IRA, $1.5 million taxable, $400,000 Roth.

At $155,000 of withdrawals, their starting rate is 3.8%. In this scenario, they fund the first years primarily from the taxable account, use partial IRA withdrawals to fill low brackets before RMDs begin at 73, and hold the Roth in reserve for large one-time costs and the surviving spouse. A two- to three-year fixed income reserve inside the portfolio cushions the sequence-of-returns risk that matters most in the first decade.

Stress-testing matters more than the point estimate. If markets fall 25% in year one, their balance drops to about $3.1 million and the same dollar withdrawal becomes a 5% rate — the plan survives, in this scenario, because roughly $40,000 of their spending is discretionary and can be paused. If neither of those things were true, the honest answer would be a bigger portfolio or a later start.

This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome.

Adjustments that move your number

  • Retiring before 65: add pre-Medicare health premiums ($20,000–$35,000/yr for a couple, illustrative) and Social Security bridge years.
  • A pension or rental income: subtract it exactly as we subtracted Social Security. $50,000 of reliable income removes roughly $1.25 million of required portfolio at 4%.
  • Inflexible spending: if all $200,000 is fixed obligation, use a lower withdrawal rate and a bigger number.
  • Legacy goals: spending down principal is legitimate; wanting to leave the principal intact pushes the requirement up meaningfully.

The bottom line

For a couple retiring in their mid-60s with typical Social Security benefits, a $200,000-a-year retirement generally implies a portfolio of roughly $3.5 to $4.5 million — less than the folk math suggests, more if you retire early, live in a high-tax state, or have no spending flexibility. The reliable method is the four-step order: rebuild the spending number, gross it up for taxes based on your actual account mix, subtract guaranteed income, then divide by a withdrawal rate that matches your horizon. Run it with your real numbers and the fog lifts quickly.
