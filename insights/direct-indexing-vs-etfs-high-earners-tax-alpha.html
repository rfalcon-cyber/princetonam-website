Direct Indexing vs. ETFs for High Earners: The Real Tax Alpha

Investing · Princeton Asset Management

You earn well into the top brackets. Every year you realize gains — from equity compensation, a business sale, a rebalance — and every April the bill stings. Now someone is pitching you direct indexing with the phrase "tax alpha," and you can't tell whether it's a genuine tool or the industry's latest wrapper for the same old fees.

It's a genuine tool — for a specific kind of investor. Here is how it actually works, what it realistically produces, and who should keep their ETFs.

First, be precise about "tax alpha"

"Tax alpha" is a marketing term. What direct indexing can generate is potential tax savings: harvested capital losses that could offset gains you realize elsewhere, deferring tax you would otherwise pay now. It is not investment outperformance, it does not make the portfolio grow faster before tax, and most of the benefit is deferral — the cost basis of your portfolio falls as you harvest, so some of the tax comes due later unless the assets are eventually donated, bequeathed with a step-up, or offset again. Keep that framing and the rest of the analysis gets much easier.

The mechanics: losses at the security level

An S&P 500 ETF is one line item. In a year the index finishes up 10%, that line shows a gain, and there is nothing to harvest — even though, inside the index, dozens of stocks may have fallen 20% or more.

Direct indexing replaces the single ETF with 200–400 individual stocks sampled to track the index. Now the dispersion is yours to use. When an individual holding drops below your cost basis, the manager sells it, books the capital loss, and buys a similar-but-not-identical stock to maintain index exposure while respecting the wash-sale rule (no repurchase of a "substantially identical" security within 30 days).

The index can be up on the year while your account harvests meaningful losses, because harvesting runs on the dispersion within the index, not on the index's direction. Harvesting is richest in the first several years, while lots are new; as the portfolio appreciates, fewer positions sit below basis and the harvest naturally decays unless you add fresh cash.

What the harvest could be worth: a worked illustration

A hypothetical: a physician couple, top federal bracket, opens a $1,000,000 direct-indexed account in 2026. Illustrative assumptions, all subject to change:

  • Losses harvested in year one: 5% of account value = $50,000 (a mid-range figure; published studies commonly discuss first-year harvests in the low-to-high single digits depending on market volatility)
  • Their tax rate on gains being offset: 23.8% (illustrative 20% top long-term capital gains rate + 3.8% net investment income tax); short-term gains offset at up to 40.8%
  • Gains available to offset: $50,000 of long-term gains from a concentrated stock sale

Potential year-one tax deferral: $50,000 × 23.8% ≈ $11,900, or about 1.19% of the account. If the losses instead offset short-term gains, the figure could approach $20,400. If they had no gains to offset, only $3,000 could be used against ordinary income this year, with the rest carried forward.

This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome. And remember the deferral point: the harvested positions now carry lower basis, so a portion of this benefit is timing, not permanent savings — though deferral itself has real value, and appreciated low-basis lots pair well with charitable giving.

The costs: fees and tracking error

Two real costs sit against the tax benefit.

Fees. Broad index ETFs are nearly free to own. Direct indexing involves a managed account, which costs more — how much varies by provider and account size. The relevant comparison is the incremental annual cost versus the potential annual tax savings, in dollars, for your account.

Tracking error. A 300-stock sample plus constant harvesting will not match the index exactly. Well-run programs typically target tracking error in the neighborhood of 0.5%–1.5% per year. Some years the account will trail the index; some years it will lead. That is not a flaw, but you must be able to look at a statement that lags the S&P 500 by a point and not fire the strategy — abandoning it after harvesting has lowered your basis is the worst of both worlds.

There is also a lock-in effect: after years of harvesting, the account is full of low-basis positions and hundreds of tax lots, which makes it harder to unwind or move than a single ETF.

Who it actually suits — and who should skip it

Direct indexing tends to earn its keep above roughly $500,000 in a taxable account, and more clearly at $1 million and up, when several things are true:

  • You reliably generate gains to offset. Business owners selling assets, executives with vesting equity, professionals with concentrated positions to diversify, anyone anticipating a liquidity event. Losses without gains mostly just accumulate as carryforwards.
  • You're in top brackets. At a 23.8%–40.8% rate on offset gains, each harvested dollar is worth more. Florida residents pay no state income tax; in high-tax states the case is stronger still.
  • The money is long-horizon. Deferral compounds; short horizons return the tax bill quickly.
  • You're funding with cash or high-basis assets. Transitioning a large low-basis ETF position into direct indexing can itself trigger the very gains you're trying to avoid.

Skip it if the account is an IRA or 401(k) — there is nothing to harvest in a tax-deferred account. Skip it if the taxable account is well under $500,000, where the incremental fee and complexity can eat the benefit. And skip it if you have no realistic source of gains: a plain ETF, held quietly, is already a remarkably tax-efficient instrument.

The bottom line

Direct indexing is not magic and it is not performance. It is a machine for converting market dispersion into realized losses, which could defer real tax for high earners with large taxable accounts and gains to offset. Whether the potential savings exceed the added fee, tracking error, and complexity is an arithmetic question specific to your bracket, your account size, and your gain profile — and it deserves to be answered with your numbers, not a brochure's.
