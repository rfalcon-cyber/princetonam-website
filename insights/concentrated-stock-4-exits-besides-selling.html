Concentrated Stock: 4 Exits Besides Just Selling

Professionals · Princeton Asset Management

You know the position is too big. Maybe it is employer stock accumulated over twenty years, or the residue of a company sale, and it now sits at 40 or 60 percent of your net worth. Selling it feels like triggering a tax bill you can measure in the price of a house. So you hold, and every earnings call you feel the exposure. The fear is specific: one bad year for one company could undo two decades of work.

Selling outright is one answer, but it is not the only one. Here are four exits that reduce risk without an immediate, full-position capital gain.

1. Exchange funds: trade concentration for diversification, defer the tax

Mechanics. An exchange fund (not an exchange-traded fund) pools your appreciated stock with shares contributed by other investors holding different concentrated positions. You contribute stock; you receive a partnership interest in a diversified pool. Because it is structured as a contribution to a partnership rather than a sale, no capital gain is recognized at contribution. After a required holding period — typically seven years — you can redeem and receive a diversified basket of securities, still carrying your original cost basis.

Trade-offs. Seven years is a long lockup. Fees are meaningful. Most funds must hold roughly 20 percent in qualifying illiquid assets (often real estate) to satisfy tax rules, which shapes what you own. You do not choose the resulting basket. And the tax is deferred, not eliminated — your low basis follows you.

Who it suits. An investor with a large unrealized gain, no near-term need for the money, and a primary goal of diversification rather than liquidity. Often executives past their option-exercise years, or founders post-lockup.

2. Collars and protective options: keep the stock, cap the downside

Mechanics. A collar pairs a purchased put (your floor) with a sold call (your ceiling) on the same stock. Sized correctly, the call premium you collect can offset most or all of the put's cost — a "cashless" or "zero-cost" collar. You still own the shares, still collect dividends, and have defined your worst case and best case for the life of the options.

Trade-offs. You give up upside above the call strike. A collar that is too tight can be treated as a constructive sale under Section 1259, triggering the very gain you were deferring — the spread between strikes matters and requires careful structuring. Options expire and must be re-established, at then-current pricing. And company trading policies may restrict hedging for insiders.

Who it suits. Someone who cannot or will not sell yet — pending a lockup expiration, a known liquidity event, or simply a decision — but who needs the downside defined in the meantime. It buys time; it is rarely a permanent solution.

3. Charitable remainder trusts: convert the position into income and a gift

Mechanics. You transfer appreciated shares into a charitable remainder trust (CRT). The trust — which is tax-exempt — sells the stock without recognizing immediate capital gain, reinvests the full proceeds in a diversified portfolio, and pays you (or you and a spouse) an income stream for life or a term of up to 20 years. What remains at the end goes to charity. You receive a partial income-tax deduction upfront, based on the actuarial value of the charity's remainder interest.

Trade-offs. This is irrevocable. The remainder genuinely goes to charity, so it only makes sense if philanthropy is already part of your plan. Distributions to you carry out the trust's income and gain over time under ordering rules, so the tax is spread, not erased. Setup and administration involve legal and trustee costs.

Who it suits. A charitably inclined investor in or near retirement who wants income from a low-basis position. It works best when the position is large, the basis is low, and the giving intent is real.

4. 10b5-1 plans and staged diversification: sell on a schedule, not on emotion

Mechanics. A Rule 10b5-1 plan is a written, pre-set schedule for selling — adopted when you have no material nonpublic information — that then executes automatically. For insiders, it provides an affirmative defense against insider-trading claims. For everyone else, the same discipline applies without the SEC formality: a written plan to sell, say, a fixed number of shares each quarter over three to five years, regardless of price.

Trade-offs. You pay the capital gains tax — this exit does not defer it, it spreads it. You will sell some shares at prices that later look low, and that is the point: you are trading regret risk for concentration risk. Current rules impose cooling-off periods before a 10b5-1 plan's first trade (90 days or more for officers and directors).

Who it suits. Executives and directors subject to trading windows, and any investor who knows their own tendency to wait for "one more good quarter." Staged selling can also be paced against tax brackets — realizing gains up to, but not through, a bracket or surtax threshold each year.

A worked illustration

Consider a 58-year-old executive holding $4,000,000 of employer stock with a $400,000 cost basis — a $3,600,000 unrealized gain. Selling everything this year could expose most of that gain to the 20 percent long-term capital gains rate plus the 3.8 percent net investment income tax — roughly $857,000 in federal tax in this scenario (2026 figures, illustrative and subject to change).

One combined approach, depending on her situation:

Instead of an $857,000 tax bill in one year, the immediate tax is limited to the staged slice, spread over four years, while roughly three-quarters of the position stops depending on one company's next earnings report. This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome.

The bottom line

Concentration is a risk you are paid nothing extra to take. The choice is not "sell everything and pay the tax" versus "hold and hope." Exchange funds defer tax in return for a lockup. Collars define your downside while you decide. Charitable remainder trusts turn a low-basis block into income and a gift. Staged selling spreads the tax and removes emotion from the timing. Most large positions are best unwound with a combination, matched to your basis, your timeline, and your intentions.
