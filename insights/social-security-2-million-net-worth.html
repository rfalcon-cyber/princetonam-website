Social Security at $2M+ Net Worth: Why the Usual Advice Is Wrong

Retirees · Princeton Asset Management

You have read the same advice a dozen times: delay Social Security to 70, collect the 8 percent annual increase, and congratulate yourself on the guaranteed return. But you also suspect that advice was written for someone else — someone without a $2 million IRA, a taxable portfolio, and a looming wall of required minimum distributions. Your quiet worry is that following the standard playbook cheerfully hands a six-figure sum to the IRS over your retirement.

The delay decision at your asset level is not really about Social Security. It is about taxes, and the two questions interact in ways the rule of thumb ignores.

The conventional wisdom, and where it comes from

Delaying from full retirement age to 70 increases your benefit by roughly 8 percent per year of delay, plus cost-of-living adjustments. For a healthy retiree, that is a real, inflation-adjusted increase from an issuer that cannot default in nominal terms. For the median household — where Social Security is the largest retirement asset — delay is often sound.

At $2 million or more, Social Security might be 15 to 25 percent of your retirement resources instead of 70 percent. The claiming decision stops being a survival question and becomes a portfolio and tax question. That changes the analysis in at least four ways.

The tax torpedo: how benefits drag other income into higher brackets

Up to 85 percent of your Social Security benefit is taxable, and how much depends on "provisional income" — roughly your other income plus half your benefit. As other income rises, it can pull more of your benefit into taxable territory, so one additional dollar of IRA withdrawal can make up to $0.85 of benefit newly taxable. In the phase-in range, a nominal 22 percent bracket can behave like an effective marginal rate near 40 percent. Planners call this the tax torpedo.

Here is the part the rule of thumb misses. The torpedo mostly hits households with moderate other income — the phase-in range. At $2M+, once benefits begin, your RMDs and portfolio income will likely push you straight through the phase-in: 85 percent of your benefit will simply be taxable, every year, forever. You cannot avoid that. What you can control is what else is stacked on top of it — which is a timing question.

The Roth conversion window: the real reason to delay

Between retirement and the start of Social Security and RMDs, many affluent retirees have a stretch of unusually low-income years. Those years are the best tax real estate you will ever own.

Delaying benefits to 70 keeps taxable income low precisely when you can use the room — converting traditional IRA dollars to Roth and filling the lower brackets deliberately. Using illustrative 2026 figures (subject to change), a married couple filing jointly might convert enough each year to fill the 22 or 24 percent bracket — the 24 percent bracket topping out at taxable income around $403,000 — while watching Medicare IRMAA surcharge thresholds, which begin near $220,000 of modified adjusted gross income for joint filers and add premium surcharges two years later.

Claim at 62 and you spend that room on benefit income instead. So the strongest argument for delay at your asset level is not the 8 percent credit at all. It is that delay preserves the conversion window that shrinks your future RMDs — and future RMDs are what arm the torpedo for the rest of your life.

A worked illustration

Consider a married couple, both 65, recently retired: $2.4 million in traditional IRAs, $700,000 taxable, combined age-70 benefits of about $95,000 per year.

Path A — claim now. Benefits of roughly $70,000 begin at 65. Stacked with dividends and withdrawals, their income leaves little low-bracket room. The IRAs compound untouched; at 75, RMDs begin near $110,000 a year and grow, stacked on top of fully taxable benefits — pushing them toward the 32 percent bracket and higher IRMAA tiers in their 80s.

Path B — delay to 70, convert in between. From 65 to 70 they live on the taxable account (largely return of basis and lightly taxed gains) and convert about $250,000 a year to Roth, paying tax mostly at 22 to 24 percent. By 70, roughly $1.25 million has moved to Roth. Benefits of $95,000 then begin; RMDs at 75 apply to a much smaller traditional balance. In this scenario, the couple could pay a known 24 percent now instead of a projected 32-percent-plus later, and their combined lifetime tax bill could be lower by a six-figure amount, depending on returns, tax law, and longevity.

Notice what did the work: delay was valuable because of what it made room for. This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome.

The survivor benefit: insurance, not investment

When the first spouse dies, the survivor keeps the larger of the two benefits — for life. Delaying the higher earner's benefit to 70 therefore buys the maximum possible inflation-adjusted annuity for the survivor, who will also, as a single filer, face compressed tax brackets and lower IRMAA thresholds on the same portfolio income.

At your asset level, frame this as insurance rather than yield. The higher earner's delay is usually worth it if either spouse is likely to reach the mid-80s. The lower earner's benefit, by contrast, disappears at the first death anyway — claiming it earlier, even at 62 or full retirement age, is often reasonable. Split claiming ages are a legitimate strategy the "always delay" rule flattens.

Break-even math is asking the wrong question

The classic objection to delay is the break-even chart: claim at 70 instead of 62 and you do not come out ahead until roughly age 80 to 82. Three problems with treating that as decisive:

  • It ignores taxes. After-tax break-evens shift meaningfully once the torpedo, conversions, and survivor tax brackets are included — and taxes are the largest variable at $2M+.
  • It prices longevity risk at zero. You do not buy insurance to break even. A couple at 65 faces a meaningful chance one spouse reaches 90 or beyond; delayed benefits are worth the most in exactly the scenarios where the portfolio is most strained.
  • The "invest it instead" comparison is unfair. Matching a delayed benefit's guarantee requires comparing against safe assets, not equity returns you might not get.

At the same time, delay is not automatic. Poor health, a large pension already covering fixed expenses, or a plan that depends on spending down the taxable account too fast can all argue for claiming earlier. The point is that the answer comes from your tax return, not a slogan.

The bottom line

Above $2 million, the Social Security question is a tax question wearing a claiming-age costume. Delaying the higher earner's benefit to 70 is often right — but for the survivor insurance and the Roth conversion window it opens, not the break-even arithmetic. The lower earner claiming earlier is often right too. Run the decision as one integrated projection: benefits, conversions, RMDs, IRMAA, and the survivor's single-filer future together.
