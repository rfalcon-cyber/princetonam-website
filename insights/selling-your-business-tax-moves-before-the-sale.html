Selling Your Business: Tax Moves You Must Make BEFORE the Sale

Business Owners · Princeton Asset Management

You built the company over twenty years, and a buyer is finally circling. Your quiet fear is not the negotiation — it is signing the papers and discovering, next April, that a third of the price went to taxes that better planning could have reduced. That fear is justified. Nearly every meaningful tax strategy in a business sale must be in place before the letter of intent, and some require years of lead time.

Once a deal is effectively agreed, the IRS treats many planning moves as too late. Here is what to consider while you still can.

QSBS: the exclusion worth checking first

If your company is a C corporation, Section 1202 qualified small business stock may be the single largest tax break available to a business seller. Stock acquired after certain dates and held at least five years can qualify for exclusion of gain — up to the greater of $10 million or 10 times basis per shareholder, with expanded limits for stock issued after mid-2025 under current law.

Three points matter before a sale:

  • The five-year clock. If you are at year four, a structure that defers the sale or uses a rollover under Section 1045 could preserve the exclusion. Selling a few months early can forfeit seven figures.
  • Stacking. Gifting qualified shares to non-grantor trusts for family members, done well before a sale, may multiply the per-shareholder exclusion. This is aggressive territory — it requires experienced counsel and real lead time.
  • Eligibility is fragile. Redemptions, certain asset compositions, and S-corporation history can disqualify stock. Get a formal QSBS analysis before you assume anything.

If you are an LLC or S corporation today, converting to a C corporation starts a new QSBS clock on future appreciation. That is only useful years ahead of a sale — which is exactly the point of this article.

Entity structure: the decision buyers will make for you

Buyers of S corporations and LLCs usually want asset purchases; buyers of C corporations often accept stock purchases. The difference can be enormous. An asset sale of a C corporation can be taxed twice — once inside the corporation, once on the distribution. An S corporation that converted from C status within the five-year built-in-gains window can face corporate-level tax it thought it had escaped.

Structural cleanup takes time:

  • S elections and built-in-gains periods need years to run
  • Personal goodwill arguments — selling your relationships and reputation separately from corporate assets — must be supported by facts established before the deal, not invented during it
  • F-reorganizations and holding-company structures common in private equity deals are best evaluated before the LOI locks the framework

Charitable strategies: the pre-LOI deadline is real

If you have charitable intent, the order of operations decides everything. Gift shares to a donor-advised fund or charitable remainder trust before the sale is a done deal, and the charity's portion of the gain may escape capital gains tax entirely while you take a deduction at fair market value. Wait until after a binding agreement, and the IRS's assignment-of-income doctrine can tax you on the full gain anyway.

The safe window is before a signed LOI — earlier is better, and certainly before any binding commitment. A hypothetical seller donating 10% of a company sold for $20 million could avoid capital gains tax on $2 million of value and receive a deduction of a similar magnitude, depending on AGI limits and appraisal requirements. Depending on your situation, a charitable remainder trust can also spread the retained gain over many years.

Installment sales: spreading the bracket

Taking part of the price as a seller note lets you report gain as payments arrive, potentially keeping annual income below the top capital gains bracket and the 3.8% net investment income tax stack point. Caveats: depreciation recapture is generally taxed up front, installment obligations above $5 million can trigger an interest charge, and you carry the buyer's credit risk. An installment structure is a negotiation point — which means it must be raised early, not after price is fixed.

State residency: move before, not after

Florida, Texas, Tennessee, and a handful of other states impose no personal income tax. California's top rate exceeds 13%; New York and New Jersey are close behind. A seller who genuinely changes domicile well before the gain is recognized may avoid state tax on the sale entirely.

Two warnings. First, high-tax states audit exiting sellers aggressively — domicile must be real: home, time, family, licenses, the center of your life. Second, some states tax gains sourced to in-state business activity regardless of where you live, and installment payments received after a move can carry the old state's tax with them. This is a two-year project, not a two-week one.

A worked illustration

Consider a hypothetical founder selling her company for $12 million with negligible basis. This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome. Figures use illustrative 2026 tax parameters and are subject to change.

In this scenario, planning begun years before the sale could reduce the tax cost by more than $3.5 million. Most sellers will not qualify for every element — but even one or two, in place before the LOI, could change the outcome by seven figures.

The bottom line

The tax outcome of a business sale is largely decided before the first buyer meeting. QSBS status, entity structure, charitable gifts, installment terms, and residency all share one property: they work when arranged early and fail when attempted late. If a sale is even a possibility in the next three to five years, the planning clock has already started.
