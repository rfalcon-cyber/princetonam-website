I Manage Public AND Private Markets — What Most Advisors Won't Tell You

Investing · Princeton Asset Management

You have $2 million, maybe $8 million. Your advisor keeps mentioning "alternatives" and "private access." You suspect — correctly — that some of what you're being shown is a product being sold, not a portfolio being managed. And you're not sure how to tell the difference.

Here is the uncomfortable part. Most advisory firms are structurally unable to do genuine private-market work. Not unwilling. Unable. This post explains why, and what real dual-market management looks like from the inside.

Why most advisors stop at public markets

The typical advisory practice is built around model portfolios: a handful of ETF allocations, rebalanced quarterly, applied across hundreds of households. That model is efficient for the firm. It is not built to underwrite a private credit fund or evaluate a limited partnership agreement.

Underwriting a private investment means reading the offering documents, the fund's actual loan-level or deal-level data, the fee waterfall, the key-person provisions, and the redemption terms. That takes days per fund, not minutes. A practice serving 400 households on one staff advisor cannot do it, so it either avoids private markets entirely or outsources the judgment to a third-party platform's approved list.

Outsourced judgment is not the same as management. When the diligence is someone else's, so is the conviction — and when the fund gates redemptions in a bad quarter, "the platform approved it" is not an answer you will find satisfying.

The access problem is real, but it's the smaller problem

You will hear a lot about "access" — minimums of $250,000 to $5 million, qualified-purchaser requirements, closed funds. Access barriers exist. Feeder vehicles and interval funds have lowered many of them.

But access was never the hard part. Selection is. Dispersion between private-market managers is wide: the gap between stronger and weaker funds in the same vintage year is far larger than the gap between two S&P 500 index funds, which is essentially zero. In public markets, picking the vehicle barely matters. In private markets, it is most of the decision.

An advisor who can get you into private markets but cannot independently evaluate which fund, at which terms, in which part of your balance sheet, has solved the easy problem and skipped the hard one.

Liquidity: the honest conversation

Private investments lock up capital. Drawdown funds may call capital over three to four years and return it over seven to twelve. Interval funds typically offer quarterly repurchases, often capped at around 5% of fund assets per quarter — and that cap is a ceiling, not a promise. In stressed markets, repurchase requests can exceed the cap and you wait.

A worked illustration. Suppose a household has $4 million investable and commits $800,000 — 20% — to private strategies:

If your advisor has never walked you through what happens to your plan when the private sleeve is frozen for two years, the liquidity conversation hasn't actually happened.

Fees: where the layers hide

Public-market portfolios built from index funds carry fund expenses that are, today, close to a rounding error. Private funds are different in kind, not just degree. Common structures across the market include a management fee on committed or invested capital plus an incentive fee on profits, sometimes over a hurdle. Feeder platforms and wrappers can add their own layer on top.

Two questions cut through most of it. First: what is the total annual cost, all layers combined, expressed in dollars on your commitment? Second: is your advisor paid anything — placement fees, revenue sharing, distribution fees — by the fund or platform? A fee-only fiduciary is paid by you alone, which means the answer to the second question should be a flat no. If it isn't, the recommendation and the compensation are pointing in the same direction, and that should trouble you.

What genuine dual-market management looks like

When one firm actually manages both sides, the pieces talk to each other:

  • Sizing comes from the plan, not the product. The private allocation is derived from your spending needs, tax picture, and time horizon — then funds are selected to fill it. Not the reverse.
  • The public side is built around the private side. If 20% of the portfolio is illiquid, the public fixed income sleeve carries more of the liquidity burden and is managed accordingly — maturities laddered against capital calls, not just against a benchmark.
  • Capital calls are planned, not scrambled. Committed-but-uncalled capital sits in short Treasuries earning something, not in a money market as an afterthought.
  • Reporting is unified. You see one balance sheet, with private marks clearly labeled as estimates that lag by a quarter or more, not two statements that never reconcile.
  • Someone read the documents. The redemption terms, the fee waterfall, the leverage limits — reviewed by your advisor, not just the fund's marketing team.

At Princeton Asset Management, this is the model: public-market portfolios with a fixed income and credit specialization, and private-market allocations underwritten in-house, both under one fiduciary, fee-only engagement.

Questions to ask any advisor about private markets

  1. 1. How many private funds did you review last year, and how many did you decline? (A firm that approves everything it sees isn't underwriting.)
  2. 2. Do you or your firm receive any compensation from the funds or platforms you recommend?
  3. 3. Walk me through the worst realistic liquidity scenario for this fund. What happens to my plan if I can't touch this money for three extra years?
  4. 4. What is the all-in annual cost, every layer, in dollars?
  5. 5. How does this allocation change how you manage the rest of my portfolio?
  6. 6. Who reads the limited partnership agreement — you, or someone you've never met?

An advisor doing real dual-market work will answer these quickly and specifically. Vague answers are themselves an answer.

The bottom line

Private markets can play a legitimate role for households with $1 million or more — but only when the advisor can independently underwrite the funds, size the allocation from your plan, manage the public portfolio around the illiquidity, and take no compensation from the products. Most practices are not built for that. The six questions above will tell you, in one meeting, whether yours is.
