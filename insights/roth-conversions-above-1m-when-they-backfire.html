Roth Conversions Above $1M: When They Backfire

Retirees · Princeton Asset Management

You have a seven-figure IRA and everyone — your golf partner, your CPA's newsletter, half of financial media — tells you to convert it to Roth before tax rates go up. But something nags at you. You suspect that writing a six-figure check to the IRS this year, voluntarily, might be a mistake you cannot take back. Sometimes that instinct is right.

Roth conversions are a genuinely powerful tool. They are also the most over-prescribed strategy in retirement planning. Above $1 million of IRA assets, the math gets less forgiving, not more. Here is where large conversions go wrong.

The IRMAA trap: a tax that doesn't look like a tax

Medicare premiums are means-tested. Your Part B and Part D premiums are set by your modified adjusted gross income from two years prior. Cross an IRMAA threshold by a single dollar and your premium jumps a full tier — for you and your spouse, for a full year.

For 2026, illustratively, a married couple filing jointly with MAGI above roughly $218,000 pays the first surcharge tier; above roughly $750,000, the top tier can add more than $5,000 per person per year to combined Part B and Part D premiums. These thresholds adjust annually and are subject to change.

A $500,000 conversion at age 66 doesn't just create income tax. It can push a couple from the base premium to the top IRMAA tier, costing roughly $10,000 to $11,000 in additional premiums two years later. That is a real cost, and it almost never appears in the back-of-the-envelope conversion math.

Bracket-fill errors: converting into the wrong tax

The standard advice is to "fill up" your current bracket. The error is filling the wrong one — or ignoring everything stacked on top.

A large conversion doesn't just face ordinary rates. It can:

  • Push qualified dividends and long-term gains from the 15% capital gains bracket into 20%
  • Trigger the 3.8% net investment income tax on your taxable portfolio income
  • Phase out deductions and credits you were counting on
  • Convert dollars at 35% or 37% that would have been withdrawn at 24% or lower in retirement

The question is never "what bracket am I in?" It is "what is the marginal rate on the next converted dollar, counting every interaction?" For large IRAs, that all-in marginal rate frequently exceeds the rate your heirs or your future self would actually pay.

The widow's penalty: the argument that cuts both ways

The strongest case for converting is the survivor scenario. When one spouse dies, the survivor files single. Same income, roughly half the bracket space. A widow drawing required distributions from a $2.5 million IRA can find herself in the 35% bracket where the couple paid 24%.

Converting while both spouses are alive — with married-filing-jointly brackets — genuinely addresses this. But the widow's penalty is an argument for measured conversions over many years, not a lump conversion at 35% today to avoid a possible 35% later. If you convert at the same or a higher rate than the survivor would face, you have paid the penalty early and added IRMAA on top.

A worked illustration

Consider a hypothetical couple, both 65, with a $2,000,000 traditional IRA and $150,000 of other annual income. This is a hypothetical example for illustration; it does not depict an actual client or guarantee any outcome. All figures use illustrative 2026 tax parameters and are subject to change.

Option A — convert $1,000,000 this year:

ItemIllustrative amount
Conversion income added$1,000,000
Federal tax on conversion (much of it at 35–37%)~$345,000
IRMAA surcharges triggered (top tier, both spouses, one year)~$10,500
Effective all-in rate on converted dollars~35.5%

Option B — convert $250,000 per year for four years, staying under the top of the 24% bracket and below a higher IRMAA tier:

ItemIllustrative amount
Annual conversion$250,000
Federal tax per year (largely at 22–24%)~$60,000
Four-year total tax~$240,000
IRMAA cost (modest tier, four years)~$8,000

In this scenario, the staged approach could move the same $1 million into Roth for roughly $107,000 less in combined tax and surcharges. Not because conversion is bad — because the rate at which you convert is everything.

Estate considerations: sometimes the IRA should stay traditional

Conventional wisdom says a Roth is the better asset to inherit, and under the 10-year distribution rule for most non-spouse beneficiaries, that is often true — especially for high-earning children.

But there are cases where converting is the wrong estate move:

  • Charitable intent. If part of your IRA is destined for charity — directly, through qualified charitable distributions, or at death — converting those dollars means paying tax on money that would have passed tax-free. Charities don't pay income tax; you shouldn't prepay it for them.
  • Lower-bracket heirs. If your beneficiaries are in the 12% or 22% bracket, prepaying their tax at 35% is a wealth transfer to the Treasury.
  • Step-up assets elsewhere. Spending taxable-account dollars to pay conversion tax can forfeit basis step-up on appreciated positions your heirs would have received tax-free.

When NOT to convert

As a shorthand, be skeptical of a large conversion when:

  • You are within two years of Medicare enrollment or already on Medicare, and the conversion crosses IRMAA tiers you could avoid by pacing
  • The conversion pushes dollars into the 35%+ brackets that could otherwise come out at 24% or below
  • You must pay the tax from the IRA itself rather than from taxable assets
  • A meaningful share of the IRA is earmarked for charity
  • Your heirs are likely to be in lower brackets than you
  • You may move to a lower-tax or no-income-tax state within a few years — converting before the move can hand your current state a tax it never needed to collect

The bottom line

Roth conversions above $1 million are a rate arbitrage, not an article of faith. They work when today's all-in marginal rate — federal, state, IRMAA, capital gains interactions — is lower than the rate you or your heirs would otherwise pay. They backfire when a lump conversion buys "tax-free" at 37% plus surcharges. The right answer is usually a multi-year schedule, sized annually, coordinated with Medicare timing, charitable plans, and your estate — and revisited every December.
