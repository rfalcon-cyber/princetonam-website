The 5 Questions to Ask Any Advisor Managing Over $1M

Investing · Princeton Asset Management

At $1 million and above, the advisory industry starts competing for you in earnest. The pitches sound alike, the offices look alike, and the fear underneath is reasonable: you cannot easily tell, from across a conference table, whose interests the person opposite you is legally required to serve. The wrong choice may not reveal itself for years, and by then the cost is compounded.

You do not need to become an expert in securities regulation. You need five questions, asked plainly, and the discipline to insist on plain answers.

1. "Are you a fiduciary at all times, on every account, in writing?"

This is the threshold question, and every word matters.

Some advisors are fiduciaries — legally obligated to put your interests first — in every interaction. Others operate under a suitability or "best interest" standard for some products and a fiduciary standard for others, switching hats within the same relationship. An advisor can be a fiduciary on your managed account and a salesperson on the annuity discussed in the same meeting.

So do not accept "yes, I'm a fiduciary." Ask: at all times? On every account and every recommendation? Will you confirm that in writing? A dually registered advisor giving an honest answer will say "it depends on the account" — which is itself useful information. An investment advisor registered under the Advisers Act owes you a fiduciary duty across the engagement.

There is nothing improper about the other models. But you should know which one you are in before you wire the first dollar.

2. "How, exactly, are you paid — and by whom?"

There are three broad answers, and each shapes the advice you will receive.

Fee-only advisors are paid solely by you — typically a percentage of assets under management or a flat fee. No commissions, no revenue sharing, no product payments.

Commission-based advisors are paid by product providers when you buy what they sell.

Fee-based — a term easy to mistake for fee-only — usually means both: a fee from you plus commissions from products.

The follow-up question does the real work: "Is there any way you or your firm earns money from my accounts other than the fee I pay you directly?" Ask about 12b-1 fees, revenue sharing, markups on bonds, and payments for order flow. Then ask to see it in the firm's Form ADV, the disclosure document every registered investment advisor must file. Part 2 describes conflicts in plain language. If the answer in the room does not match the ADV, believe the ADV.

None of this means commissions make advice wrong. It means you should know what the advice costs, in total, and who is paying for it.

3. "Who custodies my assets?"

Your money should not sit at your advisor's firm under your advisor's control. It should be held at an independent, third-party custodian — a large, regulated institution such as Fidelity, Schwab, or a major bank — in an account titled in your name.

This matters for a blunt reason: the major frauds in this industry have almost always involved self-custody. When the person managing the money also controls the reporting and holds the assets, the statements can say anything. With an independent custodian, you receive statements directly from the custodian, you can log in yourself, and the advisor has trading authority but cannot withdraw your funds to anywhere but accounts you own.

Two follow-ups: "Will I get statements directly from the custodian?" and "Can you ever take possession of my money?" The right answers are yes and no, in that order.

4. "Who actually manages my portfolio?"

The person selling you the relationship is not always the person making decisions with your money. At many large firms, the advisor is a relationship manager; the portfolio is run by a home-office model, an outsourced third-party strategist, or a call center you will never meet. That can be a fine arrangement — models can be well built — but you should know it going in, especially if you are paying for what was presented as tailored management.

Ask: Who makes the buy and sell decisions in my account? Have I met them? What are their credentials — CFA, CFP, and what experience behind the letters? If my situation is unusual — a concentrated stock position, a business sale, a bond portfolio built around my tax bracket — can the portfolio actually be customized, or am I assigned to the nearest model?

At $1 million or more, your situation usually has at least one feature a model was not built for. The question is whether anyone with authority over your account will notice.

5. "What happens when we disagree?"

This question is asked least and reveals most.

Suppose you want to hold more cash than the advisor recommends, or you refuse to sell a stock with sentimental weight, or you read something alarming and want to act on it. What happens? A good answer describes a process: the advisor documents the recommendation, explains the reasoning, tells you plainly when they think you are wrong, and then respects that it is your money. A poor answer is either "we'll do whatever you want" (you are hiring judgment, not an order-taker) or a system so rigid that your account cannot deviate from the firm's model at all.

Also ask about the exits: If I leave, what does it cost? Are there account-closing fees, surrender charges, or proprietary funds I cannot transfer and would be forced to sell — with tax consequences — on the way out? An advisor confident in the relationship makes leaving easy. Products that lock you in are answering the question for you.

Reading the answers together

No single answer disqualifies a firm. Together, they describe a structure. An advisor who is a fiduciary at all times, paid only by you, using an independent custodian, with named and credentialed people managing the portfolio, and a clear process for disagreement has removed most of the structural conflicts before the first recommendation is made. That is the standard independent, fee-only registered investment advisors are built around — and it is the standard we think any firm, including ours, should be measured against.

An advisor with different answers may still serve you well. But you will be relying on the person to overcome the structure, rather than the structure supporting the person.

The bottom line

Ask all five questions, in writing if you prefer: fiduciary at all times, paid how and by whom, custodied where, managed by whom, and what happens when we disagree. Request the Form ADV and read Part 2 before you sign anything. Any advisor worth trusting with $1 million will welcome the questions — the discomfort of asking them is far cheaper than a decade in the wrong structure.
